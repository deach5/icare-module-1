Tips to get a customer on side

Creating positive interactions

Research shows that it’s a lot harder to be abusive to someone you’re able to form a connection with — even if the connection is only brief.

Throughout this topic, you’ll explore tips to help you build positive customer relationships and increase your chances of having positive service interactions.

1. Build connection

Connect with your customers by treating them as individuals. Pay attention to them, make eye contact and actively listen to what they say.

Here are some ways you can build a connection with your customer:

  • Look at them/make eye contact.
  • Initiate a conversation (e.g., ‘Is it still nice and sunny outside?’).
  • Actively listen to their responses. This will allow you to build on the conversation rather than simply asking a series of questions.
  • Use non-verbal encouragers to show interest (e.g., ‘Right’, ‘I see’, ‘Wow’).
  • If you know their name, use it.
  • Match your words and tone to theirs (e.g., how formally they speak, etc.)
  • Adjust your speech to their demographic group (e.g., you wouldn’t speak to your grandmother the same way you speak to your friends).

2. Manage your emotions

Be conscious of your reactions to your customer’s emotions. Manage your response by focusing on your words, tone and body language.

Did you know that you can catch emotions from other people? It’s a phenomenon called emotional contagion, where we copy the emotions of another person.

You might have experienced this in the past when someone near you was happy or laughing and it made you smile and feel happy too.

Unfortunately, negative emotions can have the same effect.

What can you do?

When faced with a misbehaving customer, there are things you can do in the moment that will allow you to respond, rather than react. These include taking a moment to breathe or reframing your thinking. The technique you choose will depend on the situation.

If you feel your body reacting, take a moment to breathe. Focusing on your breathing will allow the rational part of your brain to think clearly and stop the ‘flight, fight or freeze’ response from taking over.

3. See it from their side

Demonstrate that you can see their perspective and appreciate their view of the situation.

Often a customer just wants to feel heard. By simply acknowledging their feelings and the situation from their perspective, you can calm them down enough to move forward in the interaction.

Here are some useful phrases to help you do this:

  • ‘I can see this situation is frustrating for you.’
  • ‘I appreciate this has caused you real stress and concern.’
  • ‘Thank you for sharing how distressing this has been for you.’

What can you do?

You’ll remember from Topic 1 that there are many causes of customer misbehaviour. Some of these are outside of your control and are often more about the customer and their circumstances.

Earlier, we explored using breathing techniques to help yourself think clearly and respond to a customer. When a customer’s misbehaviour is outside of your control, a helpful technique to use when responding is to reframe your thinking.

This technique encourages a shared understanding with the customer so that you can see the situation from their side.

Let’s take a closer look.

Breathe

1. Take a moment
It’s OK to take a few seconds to take one or two conscious breaths.

2. Respond
Collect your thoughts and focus on your words, tone and body language. This will enable you to respond, rather than react, to the customer.

Reframe your thinking

1. Take the customer’s perspective.
By behaving in a way that makes the customer feel heard, you’re engaging in the ‘reciprocation’ principle.

This means that treating someone how you would like to be treated will help create a more positive interaction.

2. Be aware that their frustration can be misdirected to you.
Reflect on your knowledge that there are lots of reasons for the way a customer behaves.

While it doesn’t excuse their behaviour, this knowledge gives you the power of perspective.

For example, think, ‘Wow, this customer is having a bad day’ rather than, ‘Why is this customer frowning at me?’

4. Be aware of your biases

We view the world through the lens of our values and experiences, which may be different to our customers. Be aware of how your biases may shape how you respond to others.

A bias is a tendency, inclination, or prejudice toward or against something or someone. Biases can be innate or learned.

In a service interaction, our biases can impact how we respond to customers, so we need to be aware of how our judgement of people can impact our behaviour.

This is social perception at play — our automatic, individual process of observing, forming impressions and interpreting the social world.

We naturally observe others (their physical features, clothing, and behaviours) and draw inferences about them (their characteristics, feelings, interests) based on our past experiences, biases and preconceived notions. While this allows us to look for similarities to help us form a connection, this process can be unhelpful, as it can lead us to make incorrect assumptions about a person.

Don’t be too hard on yourself though. We all do this. It’s just about being aware of our biases and where our attention goes.

Pay attention to what you pay attention to. Be curious and question your own biases.

5. Be yourself

The final tip to get a customer on side is to be yourself.

  • Smile.
  • Talk like a human.
  • Don’t just repeat policies and rules in response to customer queries.
  • Be genuine.

Authenticity is the biggest predictor of customer satisfaction and drives how customers see you. You can make a difference to a service interaction by what you say, do and feel.

You can make a difference to an interaction, but it’s not all on you. You have the right to be treated fairly by your customer.
