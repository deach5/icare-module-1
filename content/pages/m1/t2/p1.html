Why do customers misbehave?

Look out for triggers

Think back to a time when you may have been agitated during a service interaction. What made you feel agitated?

Were you in a rush? Was the heating on too high? Perhaps you were having a bad day and so weren’t feeling your usual self? Maybe it was a combination of these?

Everyone responds and deals with things differently. Being aware of how customers might respond to a situation, as well as how you respond to their misbehaviour, can help you manage the interaction appropriately.

What contributes to misbehaviour?

There are several factors that contribute to customer misbehaviour — some within our control and some out of our control.

Now that you know the different factors that can contribute to customer misbehaviour, let’s explore each one further.

Organisational aspects

Aspects of an organisation can be a critical factor in customer misbehaviour. These include:

  • policies and procedures (such as how the organisation manages bag checks or customer complaints)
  • training (such as whether a service worker has been adequately trained how to process a return).

All these things may contribute to customer frustration.

While you need to be aware of organisational aspects and how they can impact a customer’s behaviour, you can’t always manage or influence these factors when ‘on the job’.

Raise these issues with your manager or report them for improvement or risk identification.

Customer characteristics

A person’s genetics, psychological state, learned behaviours, and mindset can all contribute to how they behave in response to a situation.

Biology

Some people are more aggressive by nature due to biological aspects. These include:

  • genetics (height, weight, appearance)
  • hormones (increased testosterone)
  • neurological factors (low serotonin).

For example, someone who is tall and muscular is more likely to appear intimidating and therefore may choose to use their physical presence to get what they want from acting aggressively.

Background

Our background can also determine our tendency to be aggressive. This includes:

  • our early childhood experiences
  • learned behaviours.

For example, someone may have been taught this was how they got their way, or perhaps they didn’t learn how to communicate effectively.

Mindset

Our mindset can also impact our behaviour.

Someone’s attitude towards aggression (i.e., believing that aggressive behaviour is more likely to get them what they want) can act as a positive reinforcer for this behaviour.

Surroundings

Your surroundings, such as social norms, physical environment, available targets, or lack of deterrents can lead to customer misbehaviour.

This relates to what kind of behaviour is viewed as socially acceptable in a situation. For example, if other customers appear to be unhappy, an aggressive customer may see that they’re not the only person who is upset and decide to speak up.

Similarly, if no one stands up to their misbehaviour, they’re more likely to think people support their behaviour, and continue.

A stressful or unpleasant environment can increase the likelihood of aggression. If it’s too hot, crowded, noisy, etc, people are more likely to feel frustrated, uncomfortable, and physiologically aroused, which can lead to aggression.

In addition, environments that decrease the likelihood of detection (such as anonymity from large crowds, darkness or other factors) can enable and encourage misbehaviour.

Available targets can increase the likelihood of misbehaviour. For example, people are less likely to act aggressively when no one is around, as they don’t have anyone to take their frustration out on. Conversely, if a service worker is available —specifically a person who may appear vulnerable to the customer— they’re more likely to become a target for someone’s misbehaviour.

People are more likely to misbehave if they feel they’ll get away with it. If there are no security guards or security cameras visible, there’s nothing to immediately deter their misbehaviour.

On the other hand, if there are no alternatives through which to channel their misbehaviour (i.e., no complaints box or manager to escalate to), they’re more likely to misbehave to ensure they’ve been heard.

Adverse events

Adverse events include things that increase the likelihood for a customer to misbehave. These are events that trigger or provoke aggression, such as a price change the customer wasn’t aware of or the customer ‘having a bad day’ from a build-up of events (e.g., a missed bus or children crying).

As a result of the event, the person might feel anger, embarrassment, or shame, which, when matched with their readiness to respond aggressively (due to other factors already discussed), can lead to an increased likelihood of aggression.

While we all have bad days, only people with a combination of all factors are likely to misbehave in a situation. Anyone else might feel the same level of frustration but will walk away.

Be aware of factors that can trigger misbehaviour.

What happens during the interaction

What happens during the interaction with the customer is the last factor that can contribute to a customer’s misbehaviour.

This is the one you can have the most impact on, as what you say and do can have a direct influence on how the interaction progresses.

Consider this…

A health insurer’s customer call centre often takes a large volume of calls. They currently have even longer wait times due to complex queries. As each customer service representative answers the phone, how might they manage the situation?

1. ‘Thank you for your patience in the queue today. I apologise for the wait. How can I address your query today?’

OR

2. ‘Thanks for calling. What can I do for you today?’

Which do you think is more likely to stop the situation from escalating?

Everyone’s time is valuable. If you were the customer, would you prefer to have the time you spent waiting acknowledged?

You have the power to influence the situation with the customer.
