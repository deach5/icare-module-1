We’re all customers

We’re all customers – whether we’re buying a coffee, eating in a restaurant, attending an appointment, or booking a flight.

In a 2017 survey conducted of 6,000 service workers, 88% of respondents said they’d had a customer verbally abuse them in the past 12 months.

Think about a time when you were unhappy or dissatisfied with a service interaction.

  • What made you unhappy?
  • How did you respond?
  • Might you have responded differently if the interaction had taken place on a different day?

We all have the potential to misbehave, and we can all choose how we respond in a service interaction; when we are a customer as well as when we are a service worker.

Why do we call it misbehaviour?

You’re probably used to hearing the term ‘customer aggression’ rather than ‘customer misbehaviour’. But if someone were to ask you what customer aggression looks like, you’d likely have a very different answer to someone else. That’s because aggression means different things to each of us.

When we talk about ‘misbehaviour’, we’re talking about a range of negative ways a customer can act. This includes lower-level acts, such as incivility, through to higher level acts of verbal abuse or physical aggression. These misbehaviours operate on a continuum and frequently follow an escalating pattern, moving from incivility to abuse and then to aggression.

What to look out for

There are some key verbal and non-verbal signs of misbehaviour. Knowing what these are and being able to identify them will help you to manage customer interactions.

Clenched fists

Pursed lips or clenched jaw

Frowning

Glaring

Raised voice or increased pace

Leaning over someone

Now that you know some of the different verbal and non-verbal signs of misbehaviour, let’s take a closer look at these in relation to the misbehaviour continuum.

Incivility

Incivility includes rude, disrespectful, critical or dismissive behaviour.

Your customer may:

  • interrupt or talk over you
  • talk about you to other customers
  • let out exaggerated sighs
  • mumble about you under their breath.

You might notice:

  • non-verbal behaviours, such as tapping their watch
  • exaggerated movements, such as when putting their hands on their hips. 

You’re likely to feel uneasy and may:

  • avoid eye contact
  • try to shorten the interaction.

Abuse

The next level on the misbehaviour continuum is abuse.

Your customer may:

  • interrupt or talk over you
  • criticise or belittle you
  • make personal, negative comments towards you
  • swear or call you names
  • increase their pitch, pace or volume as they speak.

You might notice:

  • clenched fists and jaw
  • non-verbal behaviours become more intense.

You’re likely to:

  • feel the hairs rise on the back of your neck
  • get a sense that it doesn’t feel safe.

Aggression

At the high end of the continuum is customer aggression. Aggression involves intimidating, threatening or confronting behaviour.

Your customer may:

  • threaten your job
  • threaten your personal safety
  • yell or scream.

You might notice the customer:

  • using their body to intimidate you
  • encroaching on your personal space
  • standing over you or leaning across the counter
  • pointing in your face.

If a customer becomes aggressive, be wary of any loose items nearby, as they may throw them.

You’re likely to:

  • feel unsafe
  • feel at genuine risk.

Customer misbehaviour can be verbal or non-verbal. Be aware of the signs so that you can better manage the situation.

In summary

Remember that customer misbehaviour follows an escalating pattern. At the lower end, the customer might start by huffing and puffing to show they’re irritated, crossing their arms or using disrespectful comments before they escalate.

It’s at the lower levels of misbehaviour that you can usually do something to de-escalate the situation or prevent further escalation.
